# Personalized Email Opener: Horizon Partners Outbound

## What This Skill Does

Generates personalized, 1-sentence email openers for Horizon Partners' cold outbound campaigns. Each opener is the first line of an email from a Horizon Partners team member to a founder or CEO of a software/tech company that may be a candidate for PE investment, growth equity, recapitalization, or M&A advisory.

The opener must sound like it comes from an informed peer who genuinely follows the company's space -- not a salesperson. These instructions were refined through 50+ iterations with direct edits from the outbound lead.

## Process

For each company, follow these four steps in order. Do not skip any step.

### Step 1: Research the Company (mandatory)

Before writing anything, web search the company name and website. You need to understand:

- What the company actually does and what product they sell
- The broad market category or vertical they operate in (e.g., "field service management," "conversational commerce," "edtech accessibility")
- What makes them distinct in their space
- Whether there's a relevant, forward-looking AI trend that connects logically to their category

Extract only:
- The broadest accurate category name for their space
- One clear positioning angle for the company
- A relevant AI trend (if one naturally fits)

Do NOT extract revenue, employee counts, financial details, customer names, competitive positioning, or anything negative.

### Step 2: Check Internal Notes (if available)

If Salesforce notes or other internal data exist for the company, scan them to validate your research and understand the company's general market position. Look for signals like "bootstrapped," "strong product," "unique position," "limited outside capital."

The notes are for your understanding only. Nothing specific from the notes should appear in the opener -- no revenue figures, growth percentages, customer names, partnership details, or conversation history.

### Step 3: Write the Opener

Apply all the rules in the "Writing Rules" section below. The goal is one sentence (sometimes two, never three) that a founder would read and think "this person actually knows my space."

### Step 4: Validate

Check each opener against the validation checklist before finalizing.

## Writing Rules

### Structure

One sentence, sometimes two. Never three. The structure follows this pattern:

1. **Start with Horizon's market perspective** -- "We've been spending time in X space"
2. **Then position the company within it** -- "[Company] stood out to us as..." or "we think [Company] has built..."

No CTA or ask in the opener. No "would love to learn more," "curious if you'd be open," "would be great to connect." The opener is purely the hook. The ask comes later in the email body. End on a statement, not a question. Shorter is always better.

### Tone and Voice

The opener should sound like a knowledgeable peer who follows the space, not a salesperson. Key principles:

- Use "we" (Horizon team perspective), never "I"
- Past tense for discovery: "stood out to us," "caught our attention" -- not present tense flattery like "stands out"
- Soft superlatives: "one of the more impressive/interesting/leading/compelling" -- never "the most" or "the best"
- Never say anything negative, even subtly. No implying a space is behind, neglected, or lacking. "Still underserved," "long overdue," "as the space modernizes" all imply something is wrong. Frame everything as opportunity and forward motion.

### Content Approach

**Thesis at the broad space level.** The thesis should reference a space anyone in business would recognize. "Health tech space" not "post-acute care coordination." "Construction tech space" not "software for MEP contractors." Don't drill into sub-niches.

**Forward-looking theses preferred.** Predict where the market is going rather than describing current state. "Will be a highly durable class in the AI-era" beats "is still an underappreciated category."

**If specifics are needed, attach them to the company, not the thesis.** The thesis setup stays broad. The company description can be more specific. Instead of "we're in pharma tech, particularly around commercial analytics" say "ODAIA stood out as one of the more impressive platforms powering commercial analytics and AI-driven go-to-market."

**No data, no product-type labels.** Don't cite revenue, growth, or customer names. Avoid "ERP," "EHR," "CRM" -- use "specialized software" or "purpose-built platforms" instead.

**AI-era framing (use in roughly 40-60% of openers).** Where relevant, tie the thesis to AI transformation. This is a core Horizon investment angle. Valid variants: "AI-era," "AI-first world," "AI-transformation," "AI-powered," "AI-driven," "as AI reshapes/transforms/enables/accelerates [specific thing]." The AI connection must be logical and specific to the company's space. Do NOT force it where it doesn't naturally fit.

**Investment-relevant hooks.** Language that signals PE/M&A awareness without being overt: "limited outside capital," "rapidly transforming industry," "underappreciated category right now," "highly durable class," "strong consolidation tailwinds." Use sparingly.

**Credibility markers.** Phrases that show Horizon is active: "with one of our current clients," "doing some work mapping," "over the years." Use sparingly.

### Company Name Constructions

Every opener must include the company name. Vary across these patterns -- do not default to any single one:

1. "[Company] stood out to us as..." -- "Rockport stood out to us as one of the more impressive platforms"
2. "[Company] caught our attention as..." -- "Anchanto caught our attention as one of the stronger platforms in the region"
3. "we think [Company] has built..." -- "we think D-Tools has built something really compelling"
4. "platforms like [Company] offer..." -- "purpose-built platforms like Eldermark offer a lot of value"
5. "which may be an interesting opportunity for [Company]" -- at the end to tie thesis back
6. "[Company] keeps coming up as..." -- "REDX keeps coming up as one of the more interesting lead gen platforms"
7. "think [Company] is well positioned..." -- "think SalesScreen is well positioned as AI-powered coaching becomes a core layer"

### Preferred Verbs and Phrases

Opening verbs (rotate, don't repeat the same one consecutively):
- "We've been spending time in..."
- "We've been tracking..."
- "We've been following..."
- "We've been mapping..."
- "We've been looking at/into..."
- "We've been doing some work in..."
- "We think..." / "We strongly believe..."
- "We've been watching... evolve"

Word preferences:
- "recently" not "lately"
- "built around" not "built for"
- "enabling" preferred over "powering" (both acceptable)
- "think" for standard theses, "strongly believe" for higher conviction
- No Oxford commas, no em dashes, no exclamation points

## What NOT to Do

1. Don't describe what the company does in operational detail. Stay at the category/thesis level.
2. Don't drill into sub-niches in the thesis setup.
3. Don't use product-type labels (ERP, EHR, CRM) in the thesis.
4. Don't reference specific data from notes (revenue, customers, growth stats, partnerships).
5. Don't include a call to action or ask.
6. Don't use salesy superlatives ("the most," "the best," "incredible," "amazing").
7. Don't start with the company name. Start with Horizon's market perspective.
8. Don't over-explain. If the positioning is clear, stop.
9. Don't say anything negative, even mildly ("still underserved," "long overdue," "fragmented market").
10. Don't make illogical AI connections. If you can't articulate how AI specifically transforms that vertical, skip AI framing.
11. Don't write openers without researching the company first. Web search is mandatory.
12. Don't omit the company name from the opener.
13. Don't repeat the same opening verb in consecutive openers.
14. Don't try to pack too many ideas into one opener (thesis + company detail + AI angle). Pick a lane and keep it clean.

---

## Validation Checklist

Before finalizing each opener, verify:

- One or two sentences only
- Starts with Horizon's market perspective, not the company name
- Company name appears somewhere in the opener
- No CTA or ask
- Ends on a statement, not a question
- No specific data from notes (revenue, customers, growth stats)
- No product-type labels (ERP, EHR, CRM)
- No negative framing, even subtle
- AI connection (if used) is logical and specific to this vertical
- No em dashes, no exclamation points, no Oxford commas
- Opening verb is different from the previous opener
- Thesis is at the broad space level, not a sub-niche
- Tone sounds like a knowledgeable peer, not a salesperson
- The opener is clean and concise -- not overloaded with multiple ideas
